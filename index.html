Product Warnings

Structured, machine-readable warnings that make Shopify the system of record for product safety data.

The problem

Example

A merchant sells a scented candle in California.

Needs a Prop 65 warning (lead in wicks, soot particles). Today it's buried in free-text — invisible to channels, agents, and apps.

Why this matters

Platform fidelity

Shopify is the system of record for merch data. Warnings are a gap.

Agentic commerce

AI assistants can't surface safety info they can't query. They hallucinate or skip.

Channel syndication

Amazon, Google, Walmart have structured warning fields. Shopify can't populate them.

What we built

First-party structured compliance warnings on products. Replaces free-text hacks with a data model that flows across every surface.

✓

Code-complete V1

Structured warnings as metaobjects with ~15 platform defaults. Renders across Admin, Online Store, Shop, and all APIs. Demo

What we heard

Directional feedback from Commerce Platform Review expanded the vision.

CEO feedback — Commerce Platform Review

"Shopify should be an active compliance partner for merchants."

1

Warnings as metafields

Warnings should expand on the metafield type system as necessary — universal concepts with custom renderers across admin, storefronts, checkout, and channel exports.

2

Hierarchical taxonomy

Not a flat list — top-level categories (safety, chemical, age-restriction) with specific types underneath and jurisdiction mappings.

3

Shopify should be proactive, not reactive

When a merchant categorizes a product and sells into a jurisdiction, Shopify proactively suggests or applies relevant warnings — not waiting for the merchant to figure it out.

4

Separate warnings from informational labels

Regulatory/safety warnings (Prop 65, choking hazard) are distinct from informational labels ("final sale", "pre-order").

Structure

Taxonomy

~45 warnings across US & EU. One taxonomy to organize them.

Why a taxonomy?

For Shopify to be a compliance partner, we must give merchants a usable, comprehensive set of warnings out of the box. A taxonomy is the answer:

  • • Hierarchical — warnings group by hazard type, matching how regulations are written
  • • Extensible — easy to add more layers and branches without restructuring existing data
  • • Enables contextualization — taxonomy contains jurisdiction info, so the right warning is shown to the right buyer based on where they are
  • • Enables syndication — channels need typed warnings, not free text; a shared vocabulary makes this possible

Decision

Suggest, don't auto-apply.

Predictions are ~70% accurate today. At that error rate, auto-applying warnings to storefronts risks conversion and shifts liability to Shopify. Suggest lets merchants review before publishing.

Two sources of error

Prediction errors (~30%)

Category/attribute mapping produces the wrong warning — e.g. a plush toy flagged for choking hazard because the category matched, but the product has no small parts.

Merchant input errors

Wrong category or attribute — the prediction is correct given the input, but the input itself is wrong. Both models have this problem.

Suggest

Recommended

Shopify proposes warnings. Merchant reviews before anything reaches the storefront.

Errors caught at review time, not on storefront
Liability stays with merchant
Same pattern as category suggestions today
Requires merchant action — suggestions may be ignored

Auto-apply

Not recommended

Shopify applies predicted warnings automatically. No merchant review before storefront.

Instant coverage across catalog
~30% error rate reaches buyers — wrong warnings hurt conversion
Platform liability — Shopify applied it, Shopify owns the mistake

Two long-term signals that make warning predictions significantly more confident.

Safety signals

Merchant-declared product facts that narrow predictions from “this category might need a warning” to “this product needs this specific warning.”

Examples

contains_lead: true → Prop 65 for this product
small_parts: true → CPSC choking hazard
allergens: ["peanuts"] → specific allergen warning

UPI clustering

If a product shares a universal product identifier with others, warnings applied by one merchant benefit all sellers of the same product.

Examples

Merchant A adds Prop 65 to UPC 012345 → suggested to Merchant B
3 merchants flag same GTIN as choking hazard → high confidence

Network effect — grows in value as more merchants adopt structured warnings.

Signals make predictions explainable. “Contains small parts + intended for under 3” is verifiable. “It’s a toy” isn’t.

Stable ID policy

The stable ID is the most important governance artifact. Once issued, it's permanent.

Format

# Pattern

{jurisdiction}/{authority}/{warning_key}

# Examples

us/cpsc/choking_small_parts

us-ca/oehha/prop65_cancer

eu/ec/clp_carcinogenic

gb/fsa/natashas_law_ppds

Rules

✓

Immutable once assigned

Even if the warning is deprecated, the ID stays. API consumers may still reference it.

✓

Auto-generated by engineering

Compliance provides the jurisdiction, authority, and key. Engineering mints the ID.

✓

No re-use

A deprecated ID is never re-assigned to a different warning. Prevents historical data corruption.

✗

Never rename or restructure

If a warning moves categories, the ID stays the same. The ID is not tied to the tree path.

🏛️

The taxonomy is a regulated dataset, not a feature

It's governed more like a reference data standard than a product feature. Changes are auditable, append-only by default, and always traceable to a legal source. This is what makes it trustworthy for compliance teams, channel partners, and merchants.

Predict applicable warnings from category + market + taxonomy signals. Depends on product categorization (currently 17% adoption). Fully additive.

2

Dynamic placeholders

Warnings with product-specific details (chemical names, allergen lists). Key for Prop 65 (2028 chemical-specific regulation) and allergen warnings.

"This product contains {{chemical_name}}, known to cause cancer."

3

Safety signals

Product-level attributes (contains small parts, allergens: peanuts) that make warning predictions significantly more confident — narrowing from “this category might need a warning” to “this product needs this specific warning.”

4

Jurisdiction-targeted custom warnings

Custom warnings scoped to specific markets. Same escape hatch as V1, but market-aware.

5

Channel code mappings

Map taxonomy nodes to channel-specific enums (Amazon choking_hazard, Google energy_efficiency_class). Enables automatic feed population.
